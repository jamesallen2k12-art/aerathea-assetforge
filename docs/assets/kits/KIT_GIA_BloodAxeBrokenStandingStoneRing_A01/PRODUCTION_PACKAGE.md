`KIT_GIA_BloodAxeBrokenStandingStoneRing_A01` defines a broken, incomplete Blood Axe standing-stone ring used as abandoned-camp memory and warning dressing. It should read as the remains of an old hostile Giant camp perimeter or warning site: partial arcs, fallen slabs, snapped uprights, missing segments, disturbed ground, old ash, mud, and a few restrained oxide red cloth or paint accents.

The broken ring is not a ritual boundary, gameplay arena, combat space, objective marker, navigation/pathfinding device, route proof, collision-correct layout, encounter space, or active magic diagram. It is environmental history only: a rough reminder that the Blood Axe Tribe once occupied or marked the location and then moved on.

Blood Axe visual language must remain separate from neutral/civilized Giant culture. This kit may use harsh raider camp materials and old warning signs, but it must not redefine all Giants as Blood Axe raiders. Neutral/civilized Giant culture remains tied to hidden cave towns, master stonework, terraces, waterworks, hearth light, blue-gray civic stone, and restrained storm or rune accents.

## Silhouette Notes

The primary read is an incomplete and weathered ring: one or more broken arcs, obvious missing sections, fallen heavy slabs, and irregular ground scarring. The negative space and gaps are as important as the stones because they prevent the composition from reading as an active circle.

Primary silhouette families:

- Partial broken arcs: short runs of two to seven stones, never evenly spaced enough to imply a complete functional ring.
- Fallen anchor stones: large slabs lying low, partly sunk into mud, ash, grass, or collapsed base stones.
- Snapped upright remnants: shorter jagged stones with broken crowns and broad chipped faces.
- Missing segments: deliberate absent stone beats with disturbed ground, old sockets, scraped earth, or ash stains.
- Gap markers: non-gameplay visual beats such as heavy chock stones, low ash berms, old cloth scraps, or ground discoloration that clarify a break without implying a waypoint or entrance.
- Review-only layout rows: non-shipping rows for comparing broken-ring rhythm, visible gaps, abandoned-camp memory, scale, and Blood Axe/civilized Giant separation.

Model future large stone bodies, broad fractured crowns, heavy fallen slab profiles, base chock stones, major mud/ash berms, thick rope or rawhide lashings, broad static cloth strips, and large blackened iron clamps as geometry if a later task approves production. Keep fine cracks, pitting, soot speckles, ash flecks, mud streaks, cloth weave, rope fibers, lichen, scratches, and small paint chips in textures, normals, AO, or shared trim notes.

Avoid clean obelisks, perfect circles, symmetrical magical diagrams, readable rune text, UI arrows, route icons, active arena outlines, glowing channels, dense trophy clutter, graphic gore, polished blue-gray civic masonry, terrace/waterwork motifs, warm hearth symbols, and refined neutral Giant stoneworker language.

## LOD Plan

All future important static meshes need LOD0-LOD3.

- LOD0: full broken-ring silhouettes, fallen slab profiles, snapped upright crowns, partial arc rhythm, base chock stones, ash/mud ground contact, broad cloth/lashing shapes, major blackened iron clamps, and clear missing-segment negative space.
- LOD1: 60-70 percent of LOD0; reduce small bevels, secondary chips, cloth edge cuts, lashing subdivisions, small horn cuts, minor base undercuts, and hidden backside detail.
- LOD2: 35-45 percent of LOD0; simplify stones into larger planes, reduce chock stones, flatten shallow fractures, simplify cloth tears, remove most non-silhouette accent pieces, and preserve only major gap reads.
- LOD3: 15-25 percent of LOD0; preserve partial arc silhouette, fallen slab mass, snapped upright heights, visible missing segments, and a few Blood Axe red accents only.

Reduction order:

1. Tiny cracks, small chips, cloth weave, rope fibers, soot speckles, ash flecks, lichen specks, paint chips, and minor scratches.
2. Small lashings, tiny horn chips, little stone wedges, secondary cloth tears, and small metal nicks.
3. Back-facing chock stones, underside slab bevels, hidden base cuts, and non-visible ash/mud undercuts.
4. Secondary marker tokens, small iron clamps, minor bone or horn accents, and small cloth knots.
5. Stone bevel density, secondary fracture cuts, and cloth fold geometry.
6. Only after small detail is gone, simplify the main broken arc rhythm, fallen slab reads, snapped uprights, missing-segment reads, and gap silhouettes.

Never reduce the Giant-scale relationship, abandoned-camp memory read, broken negative space, Blood Axe red warning accents, or primary stone silhouettes before removing small detail.
